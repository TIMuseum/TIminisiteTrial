Historic Buildings & Sea Level Rise

Without the redevelopment of Treasure Island many of its historic buildings would be lost to the rising Bay.

Origin of Historic Buildings

Treasure Island was fabricated as part of the Works Progress Administration (WPA), a federal program to provide jobs and create infrastructure to help bring the U.S. out of the Great Depression.

The island was designed as the future home of San Francisco’s airport, but its first purpose was to host the Golden Gate International Exposition (GGIE) 1939-1940.

The World’s Fair celebrated the completion of the Golden Gate and Bay Bridges and focused attention on the country’s increased Pacific presence due to the coming of age of the west coast.

Building One

1930's Art Moderne

Built to house the GGIE administration, Building 1 is an important American instance of the art moderne style of architecture.

Its half moon shape and plaza will welcome all to the new development, as the multimodal transportation hub-- including the ferry terminal-- is sited in front of it on the south-east corner of the island. The building is redesigned as office and commercial space and as a new home for The Treasure Island Museum.

Building 1's Future

Building 1 is at the island’s point most protected from the prevailing winds sweeping eastward from the Golden Gate. Much of the island’s ground is being raised above sea level rise projected over the next 50 years.

Sea walls are being built to protect against tide and wave surges. Additionally, new buildings will be on pads elevated above future SLR. New design for stormwater management provides drainage to contain flooding.
